01 — Concept

The Concept

In Stanisław Lem's 1961 novel Solaris, scientists discover a planet-sized Ocean that thinks—but operates on logic so alien that despite decades of research, humans cannot communicate with it or understand its purpose. It generates elaborate structures, materializes visitors' deepest memories, and maintains a stable orbit around two suns through incomprehensible means.

This project brings that entity to life: a living simulation driven by C++ physics running in WebAssembly. Beneath the visual surface, a Kelvin-Voigt viscoelastic solver models the Ocean as sentient gel—not water—that stores stress, remembers past deformations, and actively shapes its own surface. The Ocean responds to observation, but unpredictably. It creates formations that emerge and dissolve on their own timeline. It appears to perform planetary-scale work we can only glimpse through surface shimmer.

You're studying it. But it's also studying you.

The usual "alien" in games and digital media is a familiar system in a strange skin. Solaris is after something harder: a mind that runs on genuinely different logic. The Ocean doesn't perform for you — it observes you back, with the same detached curiosity you bring to it.

02 — Physics

Solving the Three-Body Problem

One of the most fascinating aspects of Lem's novel: the planet orbits two suns—a configuration that should be gravitationally chaotic. In 1687, Newton formulated universal gravitation and could predict the motion of two celestial bodies perfectly. Then he tried to add a third body. The math disintegrated.

For two centuries, mathematicians sought a general solution. Euler found specific stable configurations. Lagrange discovered five equilibrium points. Then in the 1880s, Henri Poincaré ended the search for a closed-form answer. He showed that the tiniest change in the starting positions could send the trajectories off in completely different directions, folding into structures — the "homoclinic tangles" — too intricate for any tidy formula to reach. It was the first mathematical encounter with what we now call chaos.

But Lem imagined something extraordinary: in the novel, the Ocean holds its own orbit through continuous mass redistribution — not solving the equations but rewriting them, treating the three-body problem not as a mathematical puzzle requiring an elegant solution, but as a control problem demanding constant intervention. The simulation doesn't reproduce that feat — the orbit here is on rails — but it stages what the effort would look like, as the next section describes.

Computing the Tidal Field

The two suns move on fixed, scripted paths — the red giant slowly (0.08 rad/s) along a 250-unit radius, the blue dwarf faster (0.12 rad/s) at 220 units. Their orbits are on rails: scripted motion, not a gravity simulation.

What the simulation computes each frame is not the orbit but its consequence: the tidal field the two suns impose on the surface. Both sun positions feed the C++/WebAssembly solver, which evaluates the tidal potential per cell across the 128×128 grid using the standard formula:

C++ / WASM — tidal force expand 
// C++ WASM — tidal potential per cell
float tidal = (3.0f * dot * dot - 1.0f) * 0.5f * sun_tidal;
 
// Only 5% of the tidal term reaches the surface as visible displacement
float leaked_force = total_tidal * 0.05f;
cell.velocity += leaked_force * dt;
 
// The other 95% never touches the orbit. It's a fiction: a stand-in for
// the labor such an Ocean would spend resisting that pull, accumulated
// only as a stress value that drives the glow.
float counteraction_effort = fabsf(total_tidal) * 0.95f;
cell.stress += counteraction_effort * dt * 0.5f;

That value is written into a texture the fragment shader reads back as a warm glow beneath the surface, brightest where the load runs heaviest. It isn't decorative — the solver recomputes it sixty times a second — and it reads exactly as intended: the Ocean straining to hold itself against two suns, its effort surfacing as light.

Visualizing the Effort

The real challenge was making that invisible effort legible. In the fragment shader I mark "engineering zones" — regions that brighten where the tidal load runs highest. When the two suns align, pulsing FBM patterns rise across the surface:

GLSL — engineering zones expand 
float redEngineering = calculateEngineeringZone(uRedSunPos, uRedSunColor, 0);
float blueEngineering = calculateEngineeringZone(uBlueSunPos, uBlueSunColor, 1);

Where the two zones overlap they blend toward purple — the signature of both tidal fields loading the same patch of surface at once, their interference rendered as color rather than resolved into any actual counter-force.

03 — Rendering

The Living Surface

The Ocean's visual surface is driven by two systems working together. Custom vertex and fragment shaders handle the rendering—multi-layered wave displacement with Fractal Brownian Motion creates a surface that never repeats. But underneath, a C++/WebAssembly simulation computes the actual physics: a 128×128 grid of cells, each tracking height, velocity, stress, strain, viscosity, elasticity, intent, and memory.

Viscoelastic Physics (C++/WASM)

The material under the Ocean's surface isn't water — it's a Kelvin-Voigt viscoelastic gel. Water in real-time graphics follows the wave equation (∂²h/∂t² = c²∇²h); the gel obeys a different constitutive law, in which each cell's stress is the sum of an elastic term set by how far it is displaced and a viscous term set by how fast it is moving. In practice the surface pushes back against being deformed, holds the load for a beat, and lets go in a damped wobble instead of a clean travelling wave:

C++ — Kelvin-Voigt viscoelastic solver expand 
// C++ — Kelvin-Voigt viscoelastic solver
float elastic_force = -elasticity * (height - rest_position);
float viscous_force = -viscosity * velocity;
float neighbor_pull = (avg_neighbor_height - height) * stress_propagation;
 
// Intent — the Ocean reaches toward a target height it sets for itself
float intent_force = 0.0f;
if (intent_strength > 0.01f) {
    intent_force = (intent - height) * intent_strength * intent_responsiveness;
    intent_strength *= (1.0f - dt * 0.5f);   // the reach decays — the Ocean changes its mind
}
 
float total_force = elastic_force + viscous_force + neighbor_pull + intent_force;
velocity += total_force * dt;
velocity *= damping;
height += velocity * dt;

The intent field is what tips the material from merely odd into alien. Each cell reaches toward a height target it sets for itself, and because that reach decays frame by frame, the Ocean commits to a direction and then abandons it — the surface moves with no external cause and no fixed goal. Memory runs the other way: the equilibrium each cell settles toward is a slow average of how it has recently been pushed around, so the surface never quite comes back to the shape it started from.

Mood-Driven Material Properties

The Ocean's mood directly changes the physics. When recoiling, viscosity drops and stress propagation increases—the gel becomes volatile and reactive. When aloof, viscosity rises dramatically—the Ocean becomes sluggish, barely responding. When curious, elasticity peaks—the surface becomes springy and reaching. These aren't visual parameters; they're material properties in the C++ solver that change how the physics behaves.

04 — Formations

Emergent Formations

Six types of structures spawn spontaneously, each with a complete lifecycle—emergence, maturation, dissolution—weighted by rarity as described in Lem's novel:

Formation Types

Symmetriads — Perfect radial symmetry, 6-12 arms, slow rotation. The most common formation.

Asymmetriads — Towering vertical structures, 50-100m height, irregular shapes that defy easy classification.

Mimoids — Structures the Ocean extrudes to imitate whatever is nearby — a neighboring formation, the suns overhead, the watcher's own outline — always imperfectly, so the copy never quite becomes the thing it echoes.

Vertebrids — Skeletal lattice structures with L-system branching, membrane webbing between ribs, and pearlescent bone-like material. They emerge frozen and are abandoned by the Ocean.

Extensors — The rarest formation. Sprawling horizontal networks with branch-like growth patterns.

Fungoids — Ghostly translucent structures the Ocean extrudes and reabsorbs. Four generative variants (Cap, Filament, Bloom, Columnar), each built from a probability-gated decision tree so every instance is architecturally unique.

JS — formation spawn weights expand 
this.typeConfig = {
    symmetriad:        { weight: 2, lifespan: [30, 40] },
    asymmetriad:       { weight: 2, lifespan: [20, 30] },
    mimoid:            { weight: 3, lifespan: [15, 25] },
    extensor:          { weight: 3, lifespan: [10, 18] },
    vertebrid:         { weight: 1, lifespan: [12, 20] },
    'fungoid-cap':     { weight: 2, lifespan: [18, 30] },

Each formation "breathes" through subtle internal motion, rendered with translucent shaders and flowing 3D noise patterns, suggesting processes we can only glimpse.

05 — Observation

Observation Response

The Ocean notices when you're watching. Raycasting detects where the camera looks at the surface, and shader uniforms create localized "awareness" that spreads from observation points. Hover over the surface and it responds with rippling patterns and increased luminosity.

But the response drifts unpredictably every 15-30 seconds. Sometimes it's curious and the surface leans in under your cursor; sometimes it's aloof and barely registers you; sometimes it's recoiling, pulling back as if the attention itself were unwelcome. You never get told which is coming. This is the sensation the piece is chasing — you're studying it, but it's also studying you.

How to Interact

Movement: WASD or Arrow Keys. When close (< 8 units), walk on the surface. When far (> 8 units), orbit around the planet.

View: Mouse drag to look around.

Zoom: Mouse wheel, or Q/Space to zoom out, E/Shift to zoom in.

Move your cursor slowly to see the Ocean "track" your movements with a delay. The longer you hover in one spot, the more intensely it responds.

06 — Interface

Research Station UI

The simulation includes a CRT-styled research station terminal, toggled with H, that treats the experience as a scientific observation session rather than a game. It's designed to feel like equipment left behind by the researchers in Lem's novel: worn, functional, and slightly glitchy.

A mission brief and logs from earlier observation sessions meet the viewer at the outset, casting them as the latest in a line of researchers — picking up the work rather than starting it.

The terminal's panels reveal incrementally: the globe readout appears after the first formation, a mood bar registering state change after the first transition — value without label — and the psi-contamination index once it crosses a threshold of 0.3. As the Ocean's behavior shifts, the log writes its own entries — a record the viewer never wrote. The psi index itself never resets; prolonged observation leaves a permanent trace.

The aesthetic is intentional. A clean, modern UI would imply comprehension. The CRT terminal implies something older, less certain — instruments built by people who expected to understand more than they did.

07 — Atmosphere

Atmospheric Effects

The simulation includes a multi-layered atmosphere: dynamic exponential fog that responds to sun position, shader-based surface mist with Simplex noise distortion, and periodic plasma eruptions—energy fountains with turbulent curl noise, temperature-based color gradients, and realistic particle physics.

The starfield uses three depth layers with realistic stellar classification (O, B, A, F, G, K, M spectral types) and subtle parallax as the camera moves. Bioluminescence manifests as internal light orbs with pulsing shaders and electric discharge effects beneath the surface.

08 — Performance

Performance

The C++ viscoelastic simulation runs in ~1-2ms per frame for the full 128×128 grid (16,384 cells). Each frame, the heightmap and stressmap are read from WASM memory through Float32Array views and bulk-copied into the GPU textures—avoiding any per-element serialization. The rendering pipeline uses a three-tier LOD system that adjusts Ocean surface complexity based on camera distance (128, 64, or 32 segments), instanced geometries for formations and plasma, and shader optimizations including varying caching and precision management.

Results: consistent 60fps on high-end desktops at 1440p, 60fps on mid-range hardware at 1080p, and 30-60fps on modern mobile devices with automatic quality adjustment. The WASM simulation gracefully degrades—if it fails to load, the Ocean falls back to shader-only rendering.

09 — Philosophy

Design Philosophy

Solaris resists easy comprehension by design. There are no objectives, no progression systems, no tutorials. The Ocean operates on its own timeline — formations emerge and dissolve without viewer input, mood states shift without announcement, responses vary without explanation. The goal is not mastery but sustained attention: a space where observation is possible but understanding is not.
