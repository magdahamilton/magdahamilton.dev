Solaris

An interactive meditation on consciousness and the unknowable

Role
Concept, Design & Engineering
Year
2025
Tech Stack
Three.js, WebGL, GLSL

The Concept

In Stanisław Lem's novel Solaris, scientists discover a planet-sized ocean that thinks—but operates on logic so alien that despite decades of research, humans cannot communicate with it or understand its purpose. It generates elaborate structures, materializes visitors' deepest memories, and maintains a stable orbit around two suns through incomprehensible means.

This project brings that entity to life: a living WebGL simulation that resists comprehension. The ocean responds to observation, but unpredictably. It creates formations that emerge and dissolve on their own timeline. It performs planetary-scale engineering that we can only glimpse through surface shimmer.

What Makes Something Truly Alien?

Most "alien" experiences in digital media are just exotic versions of familiar patterns. Solaris explores something harder: consciousness that operates on fundamentally different logic. The ocean isn't performing for you—it simply exists, and you're privileged to witness its incomprehensible work.

The Living Surface

Multi-layered wave displacement with Fractal Brownian Motion creates an ocean surface that never repeats the same pattern. Viscosity varies procedurally—some areas flow like water, others undulate like gelatin. Subsurface scattering and bioluminescence pulse beneath translucent regions.

Emergent Formations

Five types of structures spawn spontaneously: radially symmetric Symmetriads, towering Asymmetriads, undulating Mimoids, skeletal Vertebrids, and sprawling Extensors. Each has a complete lifecycle—emergence, maturation, dissolution—weighted by rarity as described in Lem's novel. They "breathe" through subtle internal motion, suggesting processes we can only glimpse.

Observation Response

The ocean notices when you're watching. Hover over the surface and it responds with localized rippling patterns and increased luminosity. But the behavior changes unpredictably every 15-30 seconds: sometimes curious, reaching toward your cursor; sometimes aloof; occasionally recoiling, as if disturbed by observation. You're studying it, but it's also studying you.

The Three-Body Problem

One of the most fascinating aspects of Lem's novel: the planet orbits two suns—a configuration that should be gravitationally chaotic. The three-body problem, proven unsolvable by Poincaré, shows that infinitesimally small changes in starting positions produce wildly different outcomes.

But Lem imagined the ocean actively stabilizing its orbit through continuous mass redistribution—treating it not as a mathematical puzzle, but as a real-time control problem. The ocean doesn't solve the equations; it rewrites them constantly.

I implemented the gravitational chaos, then showed the ocean counteracting 95% of it in real-time. The remaining 5% appears as surface shimmer—proof of invisible work being done. Engineering zones pulse when both suns align, creating interference patterns that suggest an alien intelligence performing planetary-scale calculations you can never fully witness.

The Design Philosophy

Unlike most interactive experiences, Solaris resists easy comprehension. There are no objectives, no progression systems, no explicit tutorials. The ocean operates on its own timeline. Formations emerge and dissolve without player input. This autonomy is crucial—you're not controlling anything, you're observing something that exists beyond your influence.

Not everything needs to be understood, explained, or mastered. Sometimes the goal is simply to create a space where wonder can exist—where we can confront our own inability to comprehend the truly alien.
